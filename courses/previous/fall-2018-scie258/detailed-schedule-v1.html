Tuesday, September 11, 2018

Required reading. Fara, III.1: Exploration

9:25am: Arrive at the classroom.

9:30-11am: Classroom session. The following items are resources to be used during the session. There is no need to look at them in advance.

  • Survey: During the classroom session, you will be given an opportunity to complete the second-week survey for the course.
  • Newton's Principia : Use Andrew Motte's 1729 translation of Newton's Principia to complete the following activities:
    1. (2 minutes) Look at the title page and contents. Anything interesting or surprising?
    2. (5 minutes) Click on "Axioms, or Laws of Motion," near the top of Book I. Read the italicized parts of the three laws carefully. Choose one of the three laws and translate it into your own words using clear modern English, writing your translation in your notebook. Use a mathematical formula if you wish.
    3. (2 minutes) Click on "Section I: Of the method of first and last ratios of quantities." Don't try to read or understand the text. Scroll down to the first diagram, near Lemma 2. Does this remind you of anything? If so, what?
    4. (5 minutes) In Book III, click on "Propositions I-IX (Force of gravity)." Scroll down to "PROPOSITION VII. THEOREM VII." Read just the italicized part. Scroll down to the next proposition, "PROPOSITION VIII. THEOREM VIII." Again, read just the italicized part. What is the modern name for these two propositions? Can you write down a mathematical formula that summarizes these propositions?
  • Darwin's Origin of Species : Use the wikisource.org version of the first edition for Origin of Species , published in 1859, to complete the following activities. (Important note: we are looking only at the first edition here. Darwin significantly expanded the text for later editions.)
    1. (2 minutes) The full title of this work contains 20 words. Write down the full meaning of the title in one or two sentences of your own words, using plain modern English.
    2. (3 minutes) Click on a couple of randomly chosen chapters and search the text for the words/phrases "evolution," "evolve," and "survival of the fittest." Make a note of your results.
    3. (10 minutes) Click on Chapter XI ("Geographical Distribution"). In this chapter, Darwin is trying to explain how, if life evolved from a single original location, it could spread around the world including to remote islands. Carefully read paragraphs 359-362 inclusive. Make a note of something striking or important in Darwin's (i) style of writing, and (ii) scientific and experimental technique.

Afternoon: Either complete the National Portrait Gallery assignment (HW5c) OR pursue archival research (or both).
