COMP 131 is the entry point in Dickinson's Computer Science curriculum for students with no previous programming experience. There are no prerequisites for COMP 131. If you have taken a Computer Science course or have prior programming experience please consult with your instructor to see if COMP 132 may be a more appropriate course for you.
Goals of Instruction

At the end of the course students should be able to:

  • understand elementary concepts of computer architecture, computer programming and software development, including the ability to implement and test simple object-oriented programs

  • use scientific reasoning to evaluate hypotheses about computer program outputs

  • [QR goal] comprehend and create basic numerical and/or logical arguments. For COMP 131, this means that students will use abstraction and logical thinking as problem-solving techniques.

Course Format

Class will be a mixture of lecture, discussion, and time spent working semi-independently on the computer --- completing in-class exercises, experimenting with existing programs, and completing lab assignments.

Attendance and participation: You are expected to attend every class, on time and ready to participate. It is particularly disruptive if students miss or arrive late to lab sessions since all lab activities will be completed in pairs. Please don't do it!

Homework assignments will be made on a (near) daily basis. Homework will be due at the beginning of class on the due date. All homework exercises will be collected; however, some exercises may not be graded. All homework assignments must be typed (unless otherwise specified).

Lab assignments will be due either at midnight of the day before the next lab period (programming exercises) or at the beginning of the next lab period (written exercises), unless otherwise specified. All answers to the lab exercises must be typed. Most program code will be submitted through the WebCAT on-line grading system. You should not turn in printouts of code that has been submitted through WebCAT.

Late submissions of homework and labs: Late submissions will not be accepted for credit unless arrangements are made with me in advance or in case of emergencies. Emergencies include sudden illness or injury (student or family member), or events such as automobile breakdowns, that prevent the student from completing an assignment or attending class to turn in an assignment. Events under the control of the student, such as oversleeping or appointments of any kind, do not constitute emergencies. In cases that seem questionable, I will require proof that an emergency occurred.

Partial credit will be earned for making an honest effort on homework exercises and labs. On the other hand, solutions to conceptual and computational problems involve logical reasoning, and logical reasoning is communicated using words as well as logical symbols and program code. Therefore, full credit will be earned for solutions only when they are expressed in a clear, legible, and grammatically correct fashion, and when they are accompanied by explanations of the reasoning that led to them. In particular, answers consisting of only sentence fragments do not constitute complete solutions and so will not be graded as complete solutions.

As a practical matter, you will want to back up all of your work. This could save you much frustration in case your files are accidentally corrupted or deleted. In addition, the files you create in some labs will be used in later ones; having copies of all of your work will keep you from having to re-do the work on which these labs build. Warning: Not having backed up your work is not an acceptable reason for failing to submit an assignment. Good times to make back ups are each time you finish work for a particular day, and just prior to making major changes in your programs.

Exams

There will be two 50-minute in-class (hand-written) exams and two 120-minute lab exams. See the Course Requirements above for the dates of these exams. Except in truly exceptional circumstances, discussed with your instructor prior to missing an exam, neither in-class nor lab exams will be rescheduled.

Because of the hierarchical nature of computer science as a discipline --- with each new concept building on the carefully constructed logical edifice of all that has come before it --- the exams for this course will necessarily be cumulative. You may expect, however, that all exams for this course except perhaps the final exam will emphasize the course material most recently covered.

Conditions for in-class written exams: Each in-class exam is 50 minutes long. Each student may bring into the exam a single sheet of standard letter-sized paper containing notes on one side. Apart from this single sheet of paper, no notes or materials of any kind may be consulted during the exam. In addition, no electronic devices of any kind, including calculators or cell phones, may be used. It is highly advisable to include reasoning or working in all exam answers, as partial credit for incorrect answers can only be given if your working demonstrates a correct understanding of the concepts being examined.

Conditions for lab exams: The lab exams will be held during regularly-scheduled lab periods. In these exams you will be asked to write a Java program on the computer. You may bring any written material you like to the exam, and you may access materials in your own file system and on the course web pages during the exam. However, you may not access external websites, and you may not communicate with other people during the exam (except the instructor) by any means.

The Key To Success

The single most important thing that you can do to succeed in this course is to do a little bit of work each night. Do not wait to start a homework assignment until the night before it is due! The Dickinson College Bulletin states "Credit for courses is based on the assumption that at least three hours of study accompany each class period (excluding labs)." That means that if you wait until the night before the week's homework is due, you could quite reasonably have 9 to 12 hours of work to do. A much better approach, as stated above, is to do a little bit of work each night.

The assignments for this course are structured in a way that is designed to help you do a little bit of work each night. There is an individual web page for each class day. This page contains the reading assignment for that day, several practice exercises related to the material covered in class and in the reading and also the assigned homework questions. A good strategy is to do the reading and attempt the practice exercises during the evening following the class period. (Okay, you can do them on Saturday instead of on Friday night!) Then attempt the homework questions the following evening.

Doing a little work each night using the strategy just described has several benefits. First, doing the reading and practice exercises right away means that the material from class is fresh in your mind. It also means that if you still find something confusing after both the class and the reading, or if you find the practice exercises difficult, you will have time to consult with a TA or with your professor before attempting the homework questions the next night. Talking with a TA or with your professor for 10 or 15 minutes could save you hours of struggling with a problem that has you stumped. Thus, by working a little bit each night you will usually complete the work for this course in much less time and with much less frustration than by attempting to do the work all on the night before it is due. You will also learn the material more thoroughly as you go, thus reducing the amount of time that you will need to spend studying for the exams.

Homework Collaboration Policy

Any homework you submit for evaluation must be your own. This means that any work you submit for evaluation must have been developed by you and must have been written by you. Although you should feel free to discuss strategies and approaches to assigned work with your instructor, the TAs, and/or your classmates, you must always write up your solutions alone.

To avoid inadvertently cheating --- or even the appearance of cheating --- you should not write anything down when you are discussing individual work with other students. If you have truly understood the ideas discussed, then you should have no difficulty reconstructing them, incorporating them into your solution, and writing up that solution, all on your own.

When completing homework for this course, under no circumstances should you show another student any program code that belongs to you or have in your possession code that belongs to another student.

If you consult classmates or other sources besides your instructor in completing any work for this course, then you are obligated to acknowledge your debt to these sources in writing as part of your problem solutions. Please be as specific as possible when acknowledging a source. You might say, for example, "I talked with X when working on Problem 3" or "I incorporated Y's idea about Z in my solution to Problem 5." Proper acknowledgment will not negatively affect your grade, although failure to provide it may.

Lab Collaboration Policy

You will be assigned a lab partner for each lab session. You and your lab partner should work together on the lab assignment and submit a single solution with both of your names on it. You should not collaborate with students (or others) outside your pair when working on lab assignments.

You should feel free to incorporate into your solutions code that appears in the labs or on the course web site, provided you indicate that the code is not your own and where you got it from. You may incorporate no other code into your solutions. In particular, neither code written by students who have previously taken the course, code from other texts or other instructors, code from friends or relatives, nor code downloaded from the Internet may be incorporated into your solutions.
